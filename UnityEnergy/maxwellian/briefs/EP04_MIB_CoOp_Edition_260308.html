ELECTRICAL ENERGY INSIGHTS:

On May 11, 1935, Franklin Roosevelt signed Executive Order 7037 establishing the Rural Electrification Administration. At that moment, only 11 percent of American farms had electricity. Private utilities had made a straightforward economic calculation: stringing transmission lines across hundreds of miles of sparse rural geography could not be justified by the revenue from scattered farmsteads. The physics was available. The infrastructure to carry it was not — because the market would not build it.

The cooperative model answered this gap. Under the Rural Electrification Act of 1936, rural communities organized as member-owned nonprofit utilities. Each farmer became a co-owner of the wires serving their land. The REA provided low-interest federal loans at two percent over 25 years. Communities formed, strung wire, and brought the electromagnetic field to barns, grain elevators, irrigation pumps, and farmhouses across the continent. By 1953, the program had wired more than half of rural America.

Today, approximately 900 electric distribution cooperatives serve 42 million members across 48 states, maintaining over 2.5 million miles of distribution line — roughly 56 percent of the nation's total electric distribution infrastructure — while serving only 12 percent of the customer base. This arithmetic is the key to understanding co-op rates. Where an investor-owned utility might serve 40 customers per mile of line, a rural cooperative may serve 5. Long runs, sparse load, aging infrastructure, and wholesale power purchased from Generation and Transmission cooperatives create cost structures fundamentally different from urban utilities.

On the Geometry of Cooperative Billing — Why kVA Matters More Than kW

When a Generation and Transmission cooperative sells wholesale power to a distribution co-op, it sells apparent power — kilovolt-amperes — because that is what actually flows through the transmission infrastructure. The transformer does not distinguish between the watts that perform useful work and the reactive vars that sustain the magnetic field. Both components stress the conductor. Both contribute to thermal loading. The G&T cooperative bills for all of it.

Many distribution cooperatives pass this reality directly to their commercial and industrial members through kVA demand billing. Unlike kW demand billing — which measures only real power — kVA billing captures the full apparent power: S = P + jQ, the vector sum of watts and reactive vars. A facility running at 85 percent power factor is billed on apparent power that is 18 percent larger than the real work it is actually performing. The difference is pure cost — charged monthly, every month, regardless of whether the facility ever examines its reactive load.

This is precisely where Unity Energy's measure → manage → exchange system intervenes. By correcting power factor at the point of load, the MPTS reduces apparent power demand directly — which reduces the kVA demand charge directly. In a cooperative territory billing on kVA, there is no ambiguity in the ROI calculation. The savings are on the face of the bill, line by line, month after month. The field already contains the solution. Our task is to identify the territories where the mathematics of cooperative billing makes Unity's case for us.

The US electric cooperative landscape presents a structurally favorable but operationally fragmented opportunity for Unity Energy: 894 cooperatives serving 42 million members across 56% of US landmass, yet direct kVA billing remains rare—most co-ops default to kW demand charges with power factor penalties as a secondary mechanism. This creates a two-tier targeting strategy: Priority 1 targets are the handful of co-ops with explicit kVA or transformer-capacity charges (Roseau Electric in Minnesota, San Patricio Electric in Texas), where Unity's power factor correction delivers immediate, unambiguous bill reduction. Priority 2 targets are high-demand-charge kW billers serving industrial load—Corn Belt Energy (Illinois) at $10.36/kW, Central Rural Electric (Oklahoma) at $10.25/kW, and Pedernales Electric (Texas) with combined demand charges exceeding $13/kW. The fastest path to deployment runs through Texas and the Upper Midwest, where industrial load density intersects with cooperative rate structures that monetize demand reduction.

Structural Difference from IOUs

Cooperatives are member-owned, not shareholder-owned—meaning rate structures reflect cost recovery rather than profit maximization. This creates both opportunity and friction for Unity:

  • Opportunity: Co-ops have strong incentive to help industrial members reduce demand charges, as member satisfaction drives governance. A Unity deployment that saves a manufacturing plant $15,000/month makes the co-op look good.
  • Friction: Co-ops derive revenue from demand charges. Unlike IOUs with regulatory mandates for demand-side management, co-ops may view demand reduction as revenue erosion unless positioned as member retention or economic development.
  • Access Pattern: Decision-making is decentralized—894 separate boards, 894 separate sales conversations. The G&T cooperatives (Great River Energy, Seven States Power Corporation) may offer aggregated entry points into multiple distribution co-ops.

Major G&T cooperatives to monitor: Great River Energy (Minnesota/North Dakota), Seven States Power Corporation (7-state Southeast footprint), and Cooperative Energy (Mississippi).

SECTION 3 — TOP 5 HIGH-COST CO-OP STATES

Note: State-level averages from EIA data; co-op-specific rates often deviate. Industrial rates typically differ from residential averages listed below.

1. Hawaii — 45.19¢/kWh Average

Hawaii's extreme rates are driven by imported petroleum dependency and island grid isolation. The state has limited rural electric cooperative presence—Hawaiian Electric Company (an IOU) dominates. The cooperative structure is minimal, and industrial load is concentrated in port facilities and military installations served by HECO subsidiaries.

Unity Opportunity: LOW. Minimal co-op infrastructure; IOU-dominated market requires different sales approach.

2. California — 34.26¢/kWh Average

California's rates reflect renewable integration costs, wildfire mitigation investments, and transmission constraints. Rural electric cooperatives exist (e.g., Anza Electric Cooperative, Plumas-Sierra Rural Electric Cooperative) but serve relatively small, dispersed populations. Agricultural irrigation loads are significant but seasonal. Data center construction is concentrated in IOU territories (PG&E, SCE).

Unity Opportunity: MODERATE. High rates create ROI potential, but co-op territories lack industrial density. Agricultural cold storage may be the best entry point.

3. Connecticut — 29.58¢/kWh Average

Connecticut's high rates stem from regional transmission costs, capacity market payments, and limited in-state generation. However, Connecticut has no rural electric cooperatives—the state is served entirely by IOUs (Eversource, United Illuminating). This is a non-target for the co-op strategy.

Unity Opportunity: NONE for co-op strategy. Redirect to IOU/C&I direct sales.

4. Massachusetts — 29.50¢/kWh Average

Similar to Connecticut, Massachusetts rates are driven by ISO-NE capacity costs and transmission. Municipal light departments exist but true rural electric cooperatives are absent. Industrial load (biotech, manufacturing) is served by National Grid and Eversource.

Unity Opportunity: NONE for co-op strategy. New England cooperative infrastructure is minimal.

5. Alaska — 24.89¢/kWh Average

Alaska's cooperative landscape is robust—numerous co-ops serve remote communities with high generation costs (diesel, limited hydro). Chugach Electric Association, Matanuska Electric Association, and Golden Valley Electric Association are significant players. Industrial load includes resource extraction (oil, mining) and cold storage/seafood processing. Extreme temperatures create year-round demand peaks.

Unity Opportunity: HIGH but logistically complex. Seafood processing cold storage with poor power factor in co-op territory presents strong ROI. Investigate Chugach and Matanuska rate schedules for demand charge structures.

Notable Mid-Tier: Texas (Multiple Co-ops, High Industrial Load)

While Texas average rates (residential ~14¢/kWh) appear moderate, the co-op density and industrial load concentration make it the highest-priority state by volume. Pedernales Electric Cooperative, CoServ Electric, San Patricio Electric, Magic Valley Electric, Upshur Rural Electric, and Mid-South Electric serve significant commercial/industrial members. ERCOT market structure means co-ops pass through wholesale volatility, and demand charges are substantial.

Unity Opportunity: VERY HIGH. Texas has the largest concentration of co-ops with industrial members, explicit demand charges ($5-12/kW), and in the case of San Patricio Electric, power factor penalties. Top-priority state.
