ELECTRICAL ENERGY INSIGHTS:

Florida's transformation from subtropical frontier to the third most populous state in America is inseparable from the history of electrification. In 1926, Florida Power Corporation (predecessor to today's Duke Energy Florida) began consolidating the state's fragmented local power systems. Florida Power and Light was incorporated in 1925, serving a Miami growing exponentially from the land boom of the decade. But it was the rural territories — the phosphate mining districts of Polk County, the citrus groves of the central ridge, the cattle ranches of Okeechobee — that remained in darkness until the Rural Electrification Administration reached them in the 1940s.

Florida's rural cooperatives were organized principally between 1939 and 1955, wiring territory that the investor-owned utilities had declined to serve. Lee County Electric Cooperative, Peace River Electric, Clay Electric, and a dozen others brought the electromagnetic field to agricultural operations that would become the foundation of Florida's modern economy. The cooperatives that serve those territories today carry the same fundamental cost structure: long distribution lines, sparse residential load density, and commercial and industrial members whose reactive energy consumption determines the co-op's wholesale power cost on a kVA basis.

Every one of these loads generates reactive power — the component of the electromagnetic field that does no useful work but must still flow through every transformer and conductor between the generator and the load.

In a subtropical climate where cooling loads run nine to ten months of the year, reactive energy is not a seasonal problem. It is a structural feature of the system. Florida's commercial and industrial facilities sustain high motor loads continuously, which means their power factor penalties — whether expressed as kVA demand charges, kW demand charges with power factor adjustments, or explicit kVAR penalties — accumulate month after month without interruption. The state maintains three distinct billing environments — investor-owned, municipal, and cooperative — each with different rate structures and different degrees of transparency about reactive energy cost. A cold storage operator served by an IOU may see a kW demand charge. The same operator served by a cooperative may be billed on kVA. In both cases, the physics is identical: S = P + jQ. Florida Regulatory Structure

Section 2 — Florida Regulatory Overview

Florida maintains a fully regulated electricity market. There is no retail electric choice — commercial and industrial customers are captive to their utility and cannot switch providers to obtain lower rates. This is a critical context for Unity's value proposition: MPTS-based demand charge reduction is one of the only tools available to Florida industrial customers for meaningful electricity cost management that does not require regulatory approval or utility cooperation.

The Florida Public Service Commission (FPSC) has exclusive authority over the five investor-owned utilities — FPL, Duke Energy Florida, TECO, Florida Public Utilities, and formerly Gulf Power (now absorbed into FPL/NextEra). The FPSC approves all rate cases, sets allowable returns on equity, and must approve any new rate structure or tariff modification. This means IOU rate structures, including demand charge methodologies, are locked in for multi-year periods between rate cases and cannot be easily modified by individual facility negotiation. The FPSC's November 2025 approval of FPL's $6.9 billion four-year agreement and TECO's rate increase effective 2026 signals a rising-rate environment that strengthens the ROI case for Unity across all IOU territories.

Municipal utilities (35 in Florida) are city-owned entities that set their own rates through local government processes — city councils or utility authority boards. They are not subject to FPSC jurisdiction for rate-setting, giving them more flexibility but also less transparency. They tend to have lower administrative overhead and can act more autonomously in setting commercial and industrial rate structures.

Rural Electric Cooperatives (18 in Florida) are nonprofit member-owned organizations governed by elected boards. Their rates are set internally without FPSC oversight. Many co-ops operate under kVA-based demand billing structures — the most favorable billing environment for MPTS deployment — because kVA (apparent power) directly incorporates reactive power (kVAR), meaning every unit of reactive correction achieved by a Unity system translates directly to reduced billing demand. Co-ops often serve agricultural and light industrial members with poor power factor loads, compounding the opportunity.

The absence of deregulation means demand charge avoidance through power factor correction is not a commodity that can be arbitraged away — it creates durable, recurring monthly savings anchored to a fixed rate tariff structure. In a regulated market, Unity's savings are predictable, auditble, and bankable.

Florida Power & Light (FPL) — NextEra Energy IOU kW

~5.8M customers Largest Florida IOU SE, SW, NW Florida Rate approved Nov 2025 — 4-year lock

FPL serves the largest swath of Florida — southeast (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach), southwest (Naples, Fort Myers), and the former Gulf Power territory in the Panhandle (Pensacola, Panama City). Its industrial territory includes South Florida distribution and logistics corridors, Palm Beach County industrial parks, and the growing data center corridor in East and Northwest Florida. FPL has reported 45 new data center prospects in active pipeline as of late 2025. The Panhandle territory intersects with agricultural and light manufacturing operations. FPL's commercial and industrial demand charges are kW-based per FPSC-filed tariffs; specific current $/kW demand rates for large commercial accounts require direct tariff review but are estimated in the $10–$16/kW/month range for General Service Large Demand (GSL) accounts under the 2026 rate structure. The FPSC's four-year rate settlement locks in the structure through 2029, providing Unity with a stable, known rate environment for ROI modeling.

Unity Opportunity: MEDIUM-HIGH. FPL's enormous industrial footprint — particularly data centers, logistics warehouses, and Panhandle agricultural processors — creates volume opportunity. However, FPL's kW-only billing (not kVA) means Unity's kVAR correction savings manifest primarily through reduced kW billing demand (load side effect) and kVAR penalty avoidance where tariff provisions apply, rather than direct kVA billing reduction. Best targets: large cold storage, food distribution, and manufacturing accounts in the 300–2,000 kW demand range within South Florida and the Panhandle. Long sales cycle expected at the utility level; focus on direct industrial account engagement.

Duke Energy Florida IOU kW

~1.9M customers Central & North Florida 5% industrial CAGR 2026–2030 $44/mo residential rate decrease 2026

Duke Energy Florida serves central Florida (Citrus, Hernando, Pasco, Pinellas, Polk, and Highlands counties) and portions of north Florida. Its industrial territory includes phosphate processing in Polk County (Bone Valley region — one of the world's largest phosphate mining districts), Lakeland-area manufacturing, and the expanding data center and logistics corridor in central Florida. Duke projects a 5% industrial compound annual growth rate from 2026 to 2030, the highest industrial growth projection of any Florida IOU. Commercial and industrial rates are kW-demand based; Duke's large commercial demand charges are estimated in the $10–$15/kW range. Despite a residential rate decrease in 2026 (driven by fuel cost reductions), industrial demand charge structures remain a significant cost driver for large power users. Polk County's phosphate operations are among Florida's highest-intensity industrial electricity users and present classic reactive energy profiles from motor-driven conveyor, pump, and processing equipment.

Unity Opportunity: HIGH. Duke's central Florida territory is arguably Unity's strongest IOU-sector opportunity in the state. Phosphate mining operations in Polk County carry extreme reactive energy profiles (large induction motor loads, variable speed drives, conveyor systems) and substantial demand charges. Data center load growth in Duke territory also creates opportunities for new facility MPTS specification. Approach: direct industrial account prospecting in Polk County phosphate operations and Lakeland manufacturing; Duke's industrial CAGR also suggests new facilities coming online where Unity can be specified at commissioning.
