FROM THE DESK OF CLERK MAXWELL

On the People Who Actually Built It — The Contractor and the Field

I want to say something about who actually built industrial electricity in America.

It was not the scientists.

Edison drew the blueprints. Tesla resolved the physics. Westinghouse made the commercial case. But the people who ran the conduit, pulled the cable, set the switchgear, and terminated the panels — those were the electrical contractors. They are the ones who translated physics into reality. They put the theory into the ground, into the wall, into the building. Without them, everything the scientists discovered would have remained a lecture.

There is a particular truth that emerges from installation — something you can only learn by doing it. Field behavior cannot be fully designed on paper. It can only be understood once you see it running. Every large industrial electrical system reveals, over time, a character that no specification fully anticipated. The current profiles change as the loads evolve. The harmonics build as the facility grows. The transformer runs warmer than the nameplate suggested it would. The power factor drifts in patterns the original engineer did not model.

The best electrical contractors know this. They have been inside enough facilities, over enough years, to understand that what they install today becomes the problem someone else inherits tomorrow. They carry that knowledge quietly. It shows up in how they bid, how they engineer, and which systems they recommend to the customers who trust them.

Unity is not approaching these companies to sell them a product.

We are approaching them because they speak the only language that matters in the field: what actually happens when the load comes on, what actually happens when the compressors cycle, what actually happens when a large facility runs for five years and the transformer starts heating in ways nobody planned for.

The contractor who recommends Unity in a specification is not adding a line item. They are offering their customer something rare in a large electrical project: a system that keeps telling you the truth about your field for as long as it runs.

Find the right ears. The contractors who have been in enough facilities to know what they don't know yet. Those are the people who will understand immediately — not because we explained it well, but because we described something they have already seen.

The field was always telling the truth.
We just built the instrument that finally listens.

PART 2 — Founder-Led Outreach Strategy

Key Principle: The first message should look like a technical inquiry from a founder — not a vendor pitch.

Contractor executives and engineers respond best to messages that signal technical credibility, respect for their expertise, and the possibility of collaboration rather than product sales. Volume outreach will be ignored. Founder-level technical inquiry will not.

1 Message Originates from Michael Howell

The email comes from the founder, not a sales address. Founder outreach signals that the conversation is substantive. It also signals that Unity is still at the stage where the founder is personally engaged — which reads as early-stage credibility, not weakness.

2 Short Technical Message — No Marketing

The first contact is brief and focused on electrical field behavior. No product name-dropping, no price references, no brochure language. The message must read like one engineer reaching out to another.

3 Single Ask — A Technical Conversation

The only ask is whether they would be open to a short technical discussion. Not a demo. Not a presentation. A conversation. This lowers the barrier and matches the tone of how technical professionals connect.

PART 4 — Strategic Vision

If even one major contractor begins introducing Unity MPTS into project specifications, the technology transitions from an aftermarket product into electrical infrastructure.

Contractor adoption creates three structural advantages that no direct sales campaign can replicate:

1. Unity enters new construction at the design stage. MPTS becomes part of the electrical specification before the building is built — not a retrofit after the owner feels the pain.
2. Contractors gain a differentiated capability when bidding projects. A contractor who offers Unity-integrated field harmonization can promise something most competitors cannot: a system that manages electrical behavior from day one, not year three.
3. Unity transitions from aftermarket equipment to electrical infrastructure. This is the category shift. An MPTS system specified in the contract drawings is not optional equipment — it is part of the building's electrical design. This is the fastest pathway to hyperscale adoption.

The Long View — What Contractor Adoption Actually Means

A single contractor who writes Unity into standard specifications for a class of facilities — say, cold storage, data centers, or industrial manufacturing — represents deployment at a scale that direct outreach cannot reach. The contractor multiplies Unity's reach across every project they touch. Their engineers become familiar with MPTS. Their estimators learn how to price it. Their customers learn to expect it.

This is the fastest pathway for scaling adoption of field harmonization systems across industrial facilities in Florida — and eventually nationally.
